Quantum currency workshop · step 2 · recognition · vesica piscis

Recognition is not agreement.
It is two complete circles finding their overlap.

The vesica piscis is one of the oldest geometric forms — the shape created when two circles of equal radius intersect so that the edge of each passes through the other's centre. Neither circle is diminished. Neither becomes the other. The overlap — the vesica — is the field of genuine recognition: the place where two sovereign systems actually share something.

"False alignment is thorny ground. It looks like overlap but it chokes. Genuine recognition of shared values is what makes the vesica hold."

In a team, the vesica piscis asks: where do the CEO's values and the team's values genuinely intersect? Not where everyone has agreed to say the same thing — but where they actually mean it. That overlap is the ground on which the financial model, the governance structure, and the exit strategy can all be built. Without it, everything built here is temporary.

The four states of recognition

My circle

My values, my definition of success, what I need this to be — stated honestly, not performed.

Their circle

The team's values, their definition of success, what they need this to be — named without filtering for what I want to hear.

The vesica — genuine overlap

Where both are true simultaneously. This is the ground. Everything built here holds.

False overlap

One circle trying to become the other. Agreement performed rather than felt. Thorny ground wearing the shape of the vesica.

Where does your circle genuinely overlap with the people in this room — and where have you been assuming it does?

Both parts of that question matter equally.

Why recognition comes before strategy

Steps 3 through 9 all require a shared foundation — a genuine overlap between the values and vision of everyone in the room. Without the vesica, every framework that follows is being built on assumed agreement that may not exist. The financial model, the governance structure, the exit strategy — all of these will eventually surface a values conflict if one was never named. Step 2 names it first, on purpose, before anything is at stake except the conversation itself.

False alignment — what it looks like

False alignment is the most common pattern in founding teams and leadership groups. It happens when everyone has agreed to use the same words — "impact," "growth," "sustainability," "team" — without ever checking whether they mean the same things by them. The vesica piscis exercise surfaces this precisely because it asks each person to define their own circle independently, before looking for overlap. When the circles are drawn separately and then placed together, the genuine overlap becomes visible — and so does the gap.

Recognition signals — what to listen for

Genuine overlap

People name the same values in different words — and when they hear each other, something relaxes. There's a quality of "oh, you too" rather than "yes, agreed." The recognition is felt, not just stated.

False overlap

Everyone agrees immediately and enthusiastically without specifics. Values are named at the level of abstraction that requires no commitment. The CEO's values land first and the team's values quietly reshape to match.

Missing overlap

Two circles that don't actually intersect yet. This is not failure — it is data. A team that has named a genuine values gap has more to build on than a team that has papered over one. The gap is the starting point, not the ending point.

The financial dimension of recognition

Values alignment has direct financial consequences. A team where "success" means different things to different people will make inconsistent financial decisions — not because anyone is wrong, but because they are optimising for different outcomes. One person's "enough" is another's "stalled." One person's "responsible growth" is another's "missed opportunity." The vesica exercise makes these definitions explicit before they become conflicts embedded in the financial model.

What this step tends to surface

The most common revelation in Step 2 is that the overlap is both smaller and more solid than anyone assumed. Smaller — because some of what felt like shared values turns out to be shared language for different things. More solid — because the genuine overlap, once named, carries a weight that assumed agreement never had. A team that has found their real vesica has something to build from. A team that has only ever had false alignment has been building on air.

This exercise maps your circle, then looks for genuine overlap. Do the first two questions alone, before sharing with the room. The independence is what makes the recognition real — when you haven't heard anyone else's answers yet, your own circle stays your own.
1

Your circle — values and definition of success

Write your own answers before hearing anyone else's. What do you actually value — not what sounds good to say in this room, but what you make decisions from? And what does success mean to you, specifically — what would it look, feel, and live like?

My core values

The three or four things you actually organise your decisions around. Not aspirational — observed.

My definition of success

What does "this worked" look like for you — financially, relationally, in terms of how you spend your time?

What I need this to be

What does this business, this collaboration, this work need to give you — or be — for you to say it was worth it?

2

Their circle — as you understand it

Before the room shares: what do you believe the people you work with most closely actually value? What do you think success means to them? Write from observation, not assumption — what have you seen them make decisions from?

Their core values

Based on what you've observed — not what they've said, but what they've done.

Their definition of success

What would "this worked" look like from where they stand?

What they need this to be

What does this business or collaboration need to give them for them to say it was worth it?

3

The vesica — after the room has shared

Once everyone has shared their circles: where is the genuine overlap? Not the polite overlap — the real one. What values and definitions of success are actually shared, in the same way, by the people in this room?

Genuine shared ground

Write only what felt like true recognition — the "oh, you too" moments, not the "yes, agreed" ones.

4

The gap

Where did the circles not overlap — or overlap less than assumed? Name it without judgment. A named gap is an honest foundation. An unnamed gap is a future conflict.

5

What the vesica makes possible

Based on the genuine overlap you found: what can now be built on this ground that couldn't be built on assumed agreement? What becomes possible when the shared values are named rather than implied?

The Sower question at step 2

Return to the lens: what kind of ground is the recognition you found growing in?

Genuine overlap in good soil grows. False overlap in thorny ground chokes — not immediately, but eventually, and usually at the worst possible moment. The financial decisions that follow in Steps 3 through 9 will be made from the ground named here.

Two complete circles. Neither diminished. The vesica between them — however small — is enough to build from. Start there.
